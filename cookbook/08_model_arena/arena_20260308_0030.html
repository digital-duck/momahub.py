Information entropy, also known as Shannon entropy, is a measure of the uncertainty or randomness in a message or data set. It was introduced by Claude Shannon in the 1940s as a way to quantify the amount of information in a communication signal. Information entropy is measured by the probability distribution of the symbols or values in the message. In contrast, thermodynamic entropy is a concept from classical physics that measures the disorder or randomness of energy in a system. In other words, as information becomes more random and disorganized (high entropy), it loses its value or usefulness. Here's a concrete example: imagine you receive a message with 26 letters of equal probability (A-Z). The entropy of this message is high because each letter has an equal chance of being the next one. This means that the message is highly unpredictable, and your task of deciphering it becomes more difficult.

Information Entropy, as proposed by Claude Shannon, quantifies the uncertainty or randomness in a set of data. It measures the average amount of information needed to describe an event within that set. The higher the entropy, the less predictable the occurrence of a specific event. In contrast, thermodynamic entropy refers to the degree of disorder or randomness in a physical system at a macroscopic level. In a closed system, it tends to increase over time towards maximum disorder (the Second Law of Thermodynamics). The relationship between these two concepts lies in their shared conceptual basis - both measure some form of uncertainty or disorder. Information entropy can be considered an abstract representation of thermodynamic entropy for information-based systems. For example, consider a deck of cards with 52 unique cards. In a perfectly ordered state (all cards in order), the entropy is lowest as you know exactly which card is in which position. Shuffling the deck increases its entropy because the outcome of drawing a card becomes less predictable. This increase in uncertainty mirrors the increase in disorder observed in thermodynamics.
